BLACK HAVEN
Objective
Why Black Haven Exists
"A space where fashion meets faith, identity, and intention."

Black Haven was born from a gap — not in the market, but in the conversation. For too long, modest fashion was treated as an afterthought: functional but not beautiful, religious but not refined, covered but not confident.


This journal exists to correct that.


We are a platform for women who choose intentional dressing. Who see the abaya not as a restriction but as a canvas. Who find power in presence, not exposure. Black Haven is not a trend. It is a conviction.

Every issue is a love letter to the women who dress with purpose.

Through essays, editorials, product features and real stories — we document a culture that has always existed, but rarely been celebrated.

Editor's Note
A Letter from the Editor
"This issue is for every woman who has ever been told she dresses too quietly."

When we launched Black Haven, we were asked: who is this for? The answer was always simple. It is for the woman standing in her wardrobe at 7am, choosing with intention. For the girl who was told her covering was a problem. For the designer who embroidered prayers into hems.


This first issue is a beginning. We do not claim to have all the answers — but we ask the questions no one else seems to want to ask.

"Quiet does not mean absent. Covered does not mean hidden. Black does not mean invisible."

Welcome to Black Haven. We are glad you are here.


— Layla Rahim, Editor-in-Chief

BLACK HAVEN
Essay · Identity
Modesty Is Not a Limit —
It Is a Lens

We have inherited a strange idea: that covering is concealing. That the woman who chooses a longer hem is somehow less. Less visible. Less ambitious. Less free.

What if the opposite were true?

Modest fashion, at its best, is proactive. It is a woman saying: this is my body, this is my faith, this is my aesthetic — and they are not in conflict. The abaya is not a restriction. It is a declaration.


When we speak of modesty in fashion, we are not speaking of shame. We are speaking of sovereignty. The choice to be seen on your own terms — no more, no less.

"Modesty is about what you choose to carry — and what you leave behind."

Black Haven is built around this idea. Not a uniform. A vocabulary.

Essay · Continued
On Wearing Black Like a Language
"To wear black deliberately, every day, is to choose a language that does not shout."

Black Haven exists at the intersection of cloth and conviction. Every fabric choice is a philosophy. Every silhouette is a sentence.

The garment is not decoration. It is declaration.

This spring, we explored four new silhouettes — each rooted in function, each elevated by detail. The open-front Rania. The structured Safa. The draped Malak. The minimal Huda. Four garments. Four languages of self.


We asked each designer: what does your work say? None of them said 'covered'. They said: strong. Soft. Rooted. Present.

05
On Identity
The Politics of Presence

There is a growing movement — quiet, persistent — of women reclaiming modest dressing not as obligation, but as identity. They are designers, mothers, architects, writers. They dress in layers not because they must, but because it reflects a layered inner life.


The political act is not removing the veil. Sometimes, it is keeping it. In a world that rewards exposure, choosing coverage is its own form of protest.

"I do not dress for the room. I dress for myself. The room just gets to witness it."
— Noor Al-Rashidi, designer

Black Haven is not here to preach. We are here to document. To celebrate. To give language to what so many women already feel.

BLACK HAVEN
Story · Craft
The Woman Behind the Abaya
"She is defined not by what she covers, but by what she chooses to say with it."
Noor Al-Rashidi learned to sew by watching her grandmother repair abayas in a courtyard in Jeddah. There was no fashion school in the story then — just fabric, thread, and an old woman who treated every torn seam as a wound that needed care.

"She never threw anything away," Noor says. "She believed every garment had already been worn by the woman who needed it most."
Fabric carries memory. Every abaya is a quiet manifesto.
Today, Noor's studio in Istanbul produces forty pieces a month — each one hand-finished. She does not use the word "modest" to describe her work. "I make clothes for women who are at peace with themselves. The modesty is already there. I just follow it."

BLACK HAVENStory — Pg 09
BH.
Story · Continued
The Studio at the Bosphorus
Noor opened her studio at 27, with three sewing machines and a single client — her own mother. By 30, she had a waitlist of six months. She does not advertise. "The women find me," she says simply.

Each piece begins with a conversation. Noor asks: how do you want to feel when you walk into a room? Not how do you want to look. Feel.
"A garment that does not change how you carry yourself is just cloth. I do not make just cloth."
The spring collection for Black Haven is her first editorial collaboration. She agreed on one condition: the photography would show the garments in motion — not pinned and posed, but worn and lived in.
09
Story · Reflections
On the Future of Modest Design
Noor is cautious about the word "trend". Modest fashion has been declared "trending" twice in her career. Both times, the big brands swooped in, released capsule collections, and disappeared when the spotlight moved on.

"We were never a trend," she says firmly. "We are a culture. And cultures do not need a season to justify them."
"The women who wear what I make — they were always here. They will always be here. I just make sure they have something beautiful to wear while the world catches up."
For Black Haven, Noor's story is not exceptional. It is representative. Behind every fold of fabric in this issue is a woman like her — patient, precise, and entirely unhurried.

BLACK HAVENQ&A — Pg 10
Q&A
In Conversation
"I did not start a brand. I started a conversation that the fashion industry was refusing to have."
— Sara Al-Muzaini, Founder
Sara Al-Muzaini, 34. Founder & Creative Director. Former architect. Now building spaces between cloth and identity.
BLACK HAVEN
Q&A · Founder
Sara Al-Muzaini
"Modesty, in 2025, is an act of resistance."
Question 01
Why did you feel the industry needed Black Haven?
Because every modest fashion brand I found was either apologetic or invisible. It dressed women as if they were hiding, or tried so hard to be 'mainstream' that it lost what made it meaningful. I wanted neither.
Question 02
What does modesty mean to you personally?
It means self-authorship. The decision about who gets to see what, and when. That is not restriction — that is sovereignty. My body, my language, my terms.
Question 03
What is next for Black Haven?
More issues, more voices, more countries. And eventually, a physical journal worth keeping on your shelf — something worth returning to.

BLACK HAVENQ&A — Pg 11
BH.
Q&A · Continued
More from Sara
Question 04
Who is the Black Haven woman?
She is not a demographic. She is an orientation. She is the woman who has decided — about her values, her appearance, her presence. She may be 22 or 55, in Riyadh or London. What unites her is intentionality.
Question 05
How do you respond to critics who say modest fashion limits women?
I ask them who they think is doing the limiting. The woman choosing her garment, or the culture telling her she is wrong for choosing it? Freedom includes the freedom to cover.
Question 06
What has surprised you most since launching?
The letters. Women writing to say they finally feel seen. Not by the clothes — but by the fact that someone chose to write about them with the seriousness they deserved.
